Mahatma Gandhi was India's pivotal freedom fighter, known as the "Father of the Nation," who used non-violent resistance, or Satyagraha, and the philosophy of Ahimsa (non-violence) to lead India's independence movement against British rule. His leadership inspired movements like the Salt March and the Quit India Movement and promoted social reforms against untouchability and for the use of Indian goods. Gandhi's legacy continues to influence global movements for peace, justice, and human rights. Early Life and Principles Born Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, Gujarat, India. Studied law in London and later went to South Africa, where he witnessed racial discrimination. His experiences in South Africa solidified his commitment to nonviolent protest, the philosophy of Satyagraha (truth force) and Ahimsa (non-violence). Leadership in India's Freedom Struggle Returned to India and became a leading figure in the Indian National Congress. Spearheaded major campaigns, including the Non-Cooperation Movement, the Salt March, and the Quit India Movement, mobilizing mass support against British rule. Advocated for Swadeshi, encouraging Indians to boycott foreign goods and promote domestically produced items, often wearing simple Khadi clothes. Social Reforms Championed the cause of the Indian poor and worked to abolish the caste system, particularly the practice of untouchability. Promoted self-sufficiency, education, hygiene, and overall social development in rural India. Global Impact and Legacy His philosophy of non-violence and truth influenced civil rights leaders worldwide, including Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela. The United Nations declared October 2nd as the International Day of Non-violence in his honor. Though assassinated on January 30, 1948, his life and teachings on justice, peace, and human rights remain a source of global inspiration.
